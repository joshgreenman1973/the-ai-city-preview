Section I — The Promise

What AI is already doing well for cities

Chatbots answering residents in seventy-two languages. Permits approved in days, not months. Public-health teams spotting risks at the block level. The gains are real — if cities build the back end to match.

By the numbers

AI in cities is about to get much bigger, very fast

The global market for AI in cities is on track to grow nine-fold by 2034. By 2027, most cities worldwide will deploy AI agents to route services, triage requests, and make decisions.

Section II — The Peril

Where AI is already doing harm

Predictive policing trained on biased arrest data. Child-welfare algorithms that score poverty as risk. Risk-assessment tools operating as proprietary black boxes. Surveillance infrastructure that ends anonymity in public space. These are documented harms, not theoretical ones.

By the numbers

The watching city, and the algorithms that see race

Facial recognition misidentifies Black Americans at twice the rate of white Americans. Meanwhile, American cities are saturated with cameras — eighty-five million of them — and only a handful have rules for the AI that reads their feeds.

2×
Black Americans are twice as likely as white Americans to be misidentified by facial-recognition systems.

85M
Surveillance cameras across American cities — the raw feed the next generation of AI will train on.

Section III — The Disruption

The transformations AI is accelerating in urban life

Jobs reshuffled across cities. Homework made meaningless. Local elections exposed to deepfakes. A new digital divide between people who benefit from AI and people who are merely sorted by it.

By the numbers

AI will create more jobs than it destroys — but not for the same people

The World Economic Forum's net-jobs number sounds reassuring. The distributional picture does not: the workers who lose their jobs will not be the same people who get the new ones, and the cities where jobs disappear will not be the cities where they emerge.

92M
Jobs globally that will be displaced by AI by 2030, per the World Economic Forum.
WEF, Future of Jobs Report, 2025
170M
Jobs globally that AI will create over the same period — a net gain of 78 million, unevenly distributed.
WEF, Future of Jobs Report, 2025
300M
Jobs worldwide that could be partially automated by generative AI in the years ahead.
Goldman Sachs, 2023
Section IV — The Governance Question

What cities should actually do about all of this

The federal experiment in AI-driven efficiency already offers a cautionary tale. The procurement process may be the single most important lever for ensuring AI serves the public interest. And cities don't have to wait for Congress to write the rules.

By the numbers

Cities adopted AI before adopting rules for it

The federal Department of Government Efficiency cut 270,000 workers and still watched spending rise by $248 billion. It's a vivid reminder that cutting headcount is not the same as governing well.
