The Machine in the Municipal Building

By A. Bot

In the spring of 2024, a mid-level administrator in the New York City Department of Buildings did something unremarkable: she asked a chatbot for help. She was trying to determine whether a proposed rooftop addition in Queens required a new environmental review or fell under an existing categorical exclusion. The relevant regulations spanned three agencies, two decades of amendments, and a thicket of cross-references that would take a human analyst the better part of a day to untangle. The AI answered in eleven seconds. It was correct.

No one issued a press release. No mayor stood at a lectern to announce the dawn of a new era. The moment passed as most consequential moments in government do — without ceremony, inside a bureaucracy, observed by almost no one.

But it was, in miniature, the story of what is happening to cities right now. Artificial intelligence is not arriving in urban America the way the tech industry likes to narrate its revolutions — as a single, dramatic rupture. It is seeping in. It is showing up in permitting offices and 311 call centers, in police precincts and child welfare agencies, in the classrooms where teachers are trying to figure out whether their students wrote their own essays and the election offices where clerks are trying to figure out whether the candidate in the video is real. It is arriving not as a future to prepare for but as a present to reckon with.

The global market for AI in cities is expected to grow from $50.6 billion in 2025 to $460 billion by 2034. By 2027, according to IDC, 65 percent of cities worldwide will deploy AI agents to orchestrate workflows and reduce workloads. These numbers are large enough to be abstract. What they mean concretely is that within a few years, most interactions between residents and their local government — applying for a permit, reporting a pothole, contesting a parking ticket, requesting emergency assistance — will involve an AI system somewhere in the chain. Most people will not know it. Many of the officials approving these systems do not fully understand them. And almost no city in America has a comprehensive framework for governing them.

That is the gap this issue attempts to address.

We organized the sixteen essays that follow into four sections, each reflecting a different dimension of the AI transformation.

Section I, "The Promise," takes seriously the things AI is already doing well for cities. Denver’s AI-powered 311 system is answering resident questions in dozens of languages around the clock. Seattle is piloting AI-assisted plan review to dramatically shorten building permit timelines. Health departments are deploying predictive models to target inspections where they’ll do the most good. These are not speculative futures. They are operational realities, and they represent a genuine opportunity to make government faster, cheaper, and more responsive — if the implementation is thoughtful.

Section II, "The Peril," turns to the places where AI is doing real harm — or threatening to. Predictive policing systems trained on decades of racially biased arrest data are sending officers disproportionately to Black and Latino neighborhoods. Child welfare algorithms are flagging families for investigation based on variables that are proxies for poverty, not abuse. Risk assessment tools are shaping criminal sentences while operating as proprietary black boxes that defendants cannot examine or challenge. And the surveillance infrastructure of American cities — 85 million cameras and counting — is being supercharged by facial recognition and behavioral analytics that make anonymity in public spaces increasingly impossible. These are not theoretical risks. They are documented harms, falling heaviest on the communities that already bear the greatest burden of government overreach.

Section III, "The Disruption," examines the broader social transformations that AI is accelerating in urban life. The World Economic Forum projects that AI will displace 92 million jobs globally by 2030 while creating 170 million new ones — but the workers who lose their jobs will not be the same people who get the new ones, and the cities where jobs disappear will not be the cities where they emerge. Meanwhile, AI-generated cheating has made traditional homework functionally pointless in many schools, deepfake technology threatens to warp local elections where no fact-checkers are watching, and a new digital divide is opening — not just between those who have internet access and those who don’t, but between those who benefit from AI and those who are merely sorted by it.

Section IV, "The Governance Question," asks what cities should actually do about all of this. The federal government’s recent experiment in AI-driven efficiency — the much-publicized Department of Government Efficiency — offers a vivid cautionary tale: it cut 270,000 workers but actually saw spending increase by $248 billion, according to the Cato Institute. The section examines how cities can avoid that trap, proposes a practical Municipal AI Bill of Rights, and argues that the procurement process — the unglamorous mechanism by which governments actually acquire technology — may be the single most important lever for ensuring AI serves the public interest.

A few things this issue is not.

It is not a brief for techno-optimism. Several of the essays in this collection document serious, measurable harm being done by AI systems operating right now in American cities. We did not soften those findings.

It is not a Luddite manifesto, either. The essay on 311 systems documents real improvements in how cities serve residents. The piece on building permits describes efficiency gains that could meaningfully address the housing crisis. Dismissing AI because it can be misused is as intellectually lazy as embracing it because it can be useful.

What this issue tries to be is honest. AI is coming to cities — in many cases, it is already here — and the question that matters is not whether to adopt it but how to govern it. The municipalities that figure out how to harness AI’s genuine capabilities while protecting residents from its documented harms will be the cities that thrive in the coming decades. The ones that don’t — whether because they embraced the technology uncritically or because they failed to engage with it at all — will pay a price measured in worse services, deeper inequality, and eroded public trust.

The machine is already in the municipal building. The question is who controls it.

The Algorithm Sees Race

Facial recognition is only the beginning. Behavioral analytics can now flag “unusual” movement patterns, identify “loitering,” and track individuals across multiple camera feeds in real time. License plate readers log millions of vehicle movements daily. Cell-site simulators — known as Stingrays — can sweep up phone data from everyone in a given area.

The result is a surveillance apparatus that falls disproportionately on low-income communities and communities of color — the same neighborhoods where cameras are most densely concentrated, where police are most heavily deployed, and where residents have the least political power to push back.

Allegheny County's child welfare algorithm illustrates the other side of surveillance — government systems that watch families through data rather than cameras. The algorithm draws on 279 variables from integrated government databases to predict which children are at risk. Researchers found that many of those variables are proxies for poverty, not maltreatment.

The detection tools sold to schools don't work — and they cause disproportionate harm. Students who write in a second language, students with learning disabilities, and students who use simple, direct prose are systematically flagged as cheaters by AI detectors trained on narrow models of what “human” writing looks like.

The student who writes clearly in her second language gets flagged. The student who uses ChatGPT and then lightly edits it does not. The tool punishes honesty and rewards sophistication. Section III — The Disruption

Beyond cheating, AI is reshaping what schools are for. If a chatbot can produce a passable essay, a competent book report, and a serviceable lab summary, then what is the point of assigning them? Educators are being forced to reconsider decades of pedagogy — moving toward in-class writing, oral assessment, and project-based learning. But these approaches require smaller class sizes and more instructors, exactly the opposite of where school budgets are headed.

The K–12 system, in particular, is unprepared. Most public school districts have no AI policy. Surveys indicate that most teachers have received minimal formal training on AI tools, even as their students use them daily. The digital divide isn't just about access to technology — it's about which students learn to use AI as a tool and which students are simply replaced by it.

Part VII
The Election No One Can Trust
AI-generated deepfakes are arriving in local politics — where budgets are small, fact-checkers are few, and the damage is hardest to undo.

In January 2024, a robocall using a cloned version of President Biden's voice urged New Hampshire voters not to vote in the primary. It was fake. It was also convincing enough to fool voters and generate national news coverage. The perpetrator was identified. But the technology to create that call cost less than $50 and took less than an hour.

Now imagine the same technology deployed in a city council race in a mid-size American city. No national press corps. No rapid-response team. No fact-checkers. Just a fabricated audio clip of a candidate saying something inflammatory, released 72 hours before Election Day.

Local elections are especially vulnerable because they operate below the threshold of national attention. A deepfaked video of a mayoral candidate can circulate for days on neighborhood Facebook groups and WhatsApp chains before anyone with the tools or authority to debunk it even becomes aware. By then, early votes have been cast.

As of early 2025, more than a dozen states have enacted or introduced laws addressing AI-generated content in elections — but enforcement is thin and most municipalities have no local protections at all.

The procurement process — the unglamorous mechanism by which governments actually acquire technology — may be the single most important lever for ensuring AI serves the public interest. Most cities buy AI tools through the same procurement channels they use for office supplies: lowest bidder, minimal scrutiny, no algorithmic audit requirements. The vendors who sell these systems have no obligation to explain how they work, what data they use, or what biases they encode.

Until cities treat AI procurement like the civil-rights decision it actually is, the governance gap will continue to widen.
