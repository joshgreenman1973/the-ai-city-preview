Part I
The scale of what is coming
The global market for AI in cities is about to explode. The question is not whether cities will adopt AI — it is whether they will be ready when it arrives.

In 2025, cities worldwide will spend $50.6 billion on artificial intelligence. By 2034, that number is projected to reach $460 billion — a ninefold increase in under a decade. These numbers are large enough to be abstract. What they mean concretely is this:

Honolulu launched its CivCheck AI tool for building permit pre-review in December 2025. Early results from the city's Department of Planning and Permitting show pre-review times compressed from roughly five months to about one week on eligible applications. These are launch-window numbers, not mature operations — but they suggest AI-assisted permitting could meaningfully chip away at the housing bottleneck that has paralyzed American cities for a generation.

In public health, predictive models are helping inspectors target restaurant visits and building code checks where violations are most likely. Chicago's food-inspections model reordered inspection priorities to find critical violations sooner. Louisville used air-quality sensors to track pollution patterns linked to respiratory illness. San Jose piloted AI-optimized traffic signals on key corridors.

The potential is real. But so is the question: who decides where these tools get deployed — and who gets left out?

Part III
The algorithm sees race
When AI systems learn from decades of biased data, they do not eliminate injustice. They automate it — at scale, in milliseconds, without appeals.

The COMPAS recidivism prediction tool has been used in courtrooms across the country to help judges set bail and determine sentences. A ProPublica investigation found that it labeled Black defendants as future criminals at nearly twice the false-positive rate of white defendants. Same tool. Same courtrooms. Different outcomes by skin color.

COMPAS is not an outlier. It is a pattern. Predictive policing systems trained on decades of racially biased arrest data send officers disproportionately to Black and Latino neighborhoods — not because those neighborhoods have more crime, but because they have more recorded crime, which reflects where police have historically been deployed. The feedback loop is vicious: more policing produces more arrests, which produces more data, which sends more police.

The city that never stops watching

Facial recognition is only the beginning. Behavioral analytics can now flag "unusual" movement patterns, identify "loitering," and track individuals across multiple camera feeds in real time. License plate readers log millions of vehicle movements daily. Cell-site simulators — known as Stingrays — can sweep up phone data from everyone in a given area.

The result is a surveillance apparatus that falls disproportionately on low-income communities and communities of color — the same neighborhoods where cameras are most densely concentrated, where police are most heavily deployed, and where residents have the least political power to push back.

Fifteen thousand cameras does not describe a neighborhood. It describes a perimeter. Section II — The Peril

The numbers hide a brutal geography. The jobs being created require skills that take years to acquire — machine learning, systems architecture, prompt engineering. The jobs being destroyed are the ones that sustained the middle class in cities like Scranton, Dayton, and Fresno: payroll processing, customer service, paralegal work, medical coding.

Goldman Sachs estimates that 300 million jobs globally could be partially automated by generative AI. McKinsey projects that by 2030, 12 million Americans will need to switch occupations. The retraining infrastructure to handle this does not exist.

The arms race is already lost. Instructors cannot reliably distinguish AI-generated work from student-written work. The detection tools sold to schools do not work — and they cause disproportionate harm. Students who write in a second language, students with learning disabilities, and students who use simple, direct prose are systematically flagged as cheaters by AI detectors trained on narrow models of what "human" writing looks like.

Part VII
The election no one can trust
AI-generated deepfakes have already arrived in local politics — where budgets are small, fact-checkers are few, and the damage is hardest to undo.

In January 2024, a robocall using a cloned version of President Biden's voice urged New Hampshire voters not to vote in the primary. It was fake. It was also convincing enough to fool voters and generate national news coverage. The perpetrator was identified. The cost was trivial: the AI compute to generate the clip ran to roughly a dollar and took less than twenty minutes. The operator who placed the calls was paid about $150.

Now imagine the same technology deployed in a city council race in a mid-size American city. No national press corps. No rapid-response team. No fact-checkers. Just a fabricated audio clip of a candidate saying something inflammatory, released seventy-two hours before Election Day.

Local elections are especially vulnerable because they operate below the threshold of national attention. A deepfaked video of a mayoral candidate can circulate for days on neighborhood Facebook groups and WhatsApp chains before anyone with the tools or authority to debunk it even becomes aware. By then, early votes have been cast.

As of early 2025, more than a dozen states have enacted or introduced laws addressing AI-generated content in elections — but enforcement is thin, and most municipalities have no local protections at all.

Part VIII
Two cities, one algorithm
The new digital divide is not between those who have internet access and those who do not. It is between those who benefit from AI and those who are merely sorted by it.
AI as a tool
Affluent residents use AI for tax optimization, health monitoring, personalized education.
AI helps them navigate systems — appeals, applications, investments.
They choose when to engage with AI and when to opt out.
AI as a gatekeeper
Low-income residents encounter AI as a barrier: benefits screening, fraud detection, risk scoring.
AI decides whether they qualify — for housing, child custody, parole.
They cannot opt out. They often do not know AI is involved.

The Allegheny Family Screening Tool, a child-welfare risk model deployed in Allegheny County, Pennsylvania, pulls from dozens of integrated government databases — public benefits, juvenile justice, child protective services history, jail records. Virginia Eubanks, in Automating Inequality, documented how many of those inputs are proxies for poverty rather than maltreatment: using Medicaid, receiving food stamps, having a prior child-welfare referral regardless of outcome.

Insurance companies use AI to set premiums based on neighborhood-level data. Employers use AI to screen resumes, filtering out candidates from certain zip codes. Landlords use AI tenant-screening tools that draw on eviction records — records disproportionately generated in jurisdictions that target Black and Latino renters.

Part IX
Who is watching the machine?
Most U.S. cities adopted AI tools before adopting rules for them. The gap between deployment and governance is the defining municipal-technology challenge of the decade.

The federal government's most prominent experiment in AI-driven efficiency offers a vivid cautionary tale. The Department of Government Efficiency cut roughly 270,000 workers — the largest peacetime federal workforce reduction on record. The result, according to the libertarian Cato Institute, not an organization hostile to cutting government spending, was that federal outlays rose by $248 billion over the same period.

Efficiency theater is not efficiency. Cities should take note. Section IV — The Governance Question

There is no authoritative national census of which U.S. cities are using AI or which have published governance frameworks for it. What we do know, from policy scans conducted by the AI Now Institute, the GovAI Coalition, and municipal-procurement researchers, is that the gap between deployment and governance is large and growing. Most cities using AI tools have no public framework at all — no procurement guidelines, no algorithmic-impact assessment, no community oversight board, no meaningful resident appeal.

The procurement process — the unglamorous mechanism by which governments actually acquire technology — may be the single most important lever for ensuring AI serves the public interest. Most cities buy AI tools through the same procurement channels they use for office supplies: lowest bidder, minimal scrutiny, no algorithmic audit requirements. The vendors who sell these systems have no obligation to explain how they work, what data they use, or what biases they encode.

Until cities treat AI procurement like the civil-rights decision it actually is, the governance gap will continue to widen.
